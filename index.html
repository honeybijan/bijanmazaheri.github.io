About me

I am a computer scientist and mathematician interested in hidden variables in causal structures: when and if they can be learned and how they affect networks of opinions. While machine learning is often focused on building models from data, my work explores what models can tell us about incomplete or absent data. I am pursuing a Ph.D. in Computing and Mathematical Sciences at Caltech supported by a NSF Graduate Research Fellowship and an Amazon AI4Science Fellowship. During fall 2022 and winter 2023 I am in Tübingen, Germany interning at the Amazon Research Causality Group.

I am also a competitive runner. My personal best for the marathon is 2:15:26 at the Chicago Marathon and I was one of the youngest athletes at the 2020 US Olympic Marathon Trials. In 2022, I was the USA 50km National Champion. For more information, see running info.

Research

I work with Professor Jehoshua Bruck and Professor Leonard Schulman on topics related to causality, data synthesis and mixture models:
  • Expert Graphs: We have developed a structure to study the consistency in overlapping expertise. This work helps us understand non-transitive properties in networks of machine learning classifiers for model-level data and knowledge synthesis.
  • Mixture Models for Causality: Interventional distributions acted on by an unobserveed confounder are traditionally considered unidentifiable. We use discrete mixture models to identify the within-component probability distributions, allowing access to the ``adjustment set'' needed to identify the causal relatinoship.
  • Domain Adaptation and Transportability: I am interested in using causal principles to guide models that are robust to distribution shift in datasets - both by demographic shifts and from sampling bias.

    Projects

    • Cross Country Rankings - I maintain a website that uses statistical modeling to adjust cross country times based on the difficulty of the course. The website is used to generate rankings and simulate hypothetical races. Since its release in September, the website has had over 400,000 views and 10,000 unique users. The data has been used for a number of smaller research projects by students across the US.
